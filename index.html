1. Strategic Context

An independent infrastructure originator

What StrataNode does

We design, de-risk and structure the integrated physical and digital ground-layer assets required to enable scalable, commercial UAS operations.

What StrataNode does not do

We do not manufacture aircraft, operate logistics fleets or develop standalone UTM software packages.

Core philosophy

Physical infrastructure first, regulatory compliance second and commercial viability third. Secure connectivity, trusted data and future programmable settlement remain enabling utility layers beneath the corridor network.

2. Why Now

The policy is advancing. The ground layer remains immature.

Policy transition

Following the launch of Ireland’s National Policy Framework for Unmanned Aircraft Systems in August 2025, the country has moved from consultation towards active implementation.

Regulatory credibility

In 2021, the IAA issued Skyports a BVLOS-capable Light UAS Operator Certificate described by the IAA as the first such certificate issued by an EU regulator.

International precedent

European corridor initiatives have demonstrated automated ground stations integrated with secure machine-to-machine peer-to-peer communications, while identifying tokenisation, machine payments, trusted telemetry and insurance-data infrastructure as areas for further development.

Infrastructure gap

Aircraft, software and airspace systems are developing faster than the physical, commercial and site infrastructure required for repeatable operations.

3. Policy Alignment

Aligned with stated public-policy objectives

StrataNode does not claim to represent the State. It structures private-sector projects so they can support the practical implementation of relevant national and local UAS-policy objectives.

Airspace and planning

Supports industry-led development, cross-government collaboration, geographical-zone planning and future U-space readiness.

Compliance and safety

Structures proposed projects for assessment within the appropriate IAA and EASA framework, including Specific Category, operational-authorisation and SORA requirements where applicable.

Enterprise and innovation

Aligns with Dublin City Council’s 2024–2029 Drone and Urban Air Mobility Strategy to support the wider ecosystem and innovative third-party services.

BVLOS operations remain subject to advance authorisation, including where geographical zones are intended to support drone activity.

9. Corridor Defensibility

Control comes from rights around the corridor—not ownership of airspace

StrataNode does not claim low-altitude airspace. Long-term commercial defensibility is created through contractual, physical, digital and data rights.

Site Rights

Options, leases and access agreements over origin and destination nodes, charging sites, rooftops and communications points.

Development Rights

Feasibility and scoping agreements may preserve first-refusal, continuing-development, licensing and project-participation rights, including potential future SPV participation or agreed revenue rights.

Digital Coordination

Operating or licensing the platform connecting operators, node hosts, charging systems, maintenance providers and weather networks.

Operational Data Rights

Contractual rights to process, anonymise and commercialise defined non-personal utilisation, reliability, charging and route-performance data.

Project Participation

Development fees, management fees, licensing, data revenue, SPV equity and multi-operator access or booking fees.

13. Commercial Structure

Project-specific participation

A viable corridor may be structured through an independent Special Purpose Vehicle after customer demand, aviation and planning pathways, site access, operator participation, construction requirements and project economics have been validated.

A project-specific SPV could ring-fence

  • Contractual liabilities
  • Site and land-access agreements
  • Planning permissions
  • Technology and operator agreements
  • Infrastructure assets and data rights
  • Project financing and revenue participation

Validation before structure

Where a corridor subsequently develops proven contractual revenues, the SPV may provide a structure through which private, strategic, institutional or public-sector capital can participate.

SPV formation should follow feasibility and project validation rather than precede them.

15. Risks and Policy-Informed Pre-Mortem

What could go wrong?

Market timing

Focus on immediate B2B commercial utility rather than speculative consumer adoption.

Regulatory delay

Complex or BVLOS operating concepts may require detailed, route-specific regulatory assessment and can involve extended approval timelines. Mitigation: Prioritise routes that appear capable of presenting manageable ground and air risk, subject to formal SORA analysis and regulatory review.

Connectivity and cyber disruption

Evaluate redundant communications, secure peer-to-peer networking, edge processing and zero-trust device identity during system design.

Customer uncertainty

Use the technical-scoping phase to quantify operational efficiencies before seeking binding commitments.

Public and planning friction

Prioritise transparent, benefit-led projects addressing environmental, industrial or public-good use cases.

The consultancy trap

Preserve StrataNode’s potential future role through development rights, site rights, licensing, data rights, management responsibilities, SPV participation or agreed revenue sharing.

16. Proposed First Step

One structured corridor-feasibility exercise

Technical and spatial scoping window

StrataNode proposes a defined scoping exercise for one preliminary industrial route, covering route logic, indicative ground-risk conditions, site access, customer demand, infrastructure requirements, operator needs and commercial structure.

The exercise would also include a digital-infrastructure blueprint assessing secure networking, data latency, cryptographic node identity and interoperability requirements.

Institutional guardrail

The project may be described as a potential industry-led demonstrator intended to assess whether it could become broadly aligned with the objectives of Ireland’s National Policy Framework for UAS. This does not imply government appointment, regulatory approval or State endorsement.
