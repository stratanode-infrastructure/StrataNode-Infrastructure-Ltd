1. Who StrataNode Is

An independent infrastructure originator

StrataNode designs, de-risks, and structures the integrated physical and digital ground-layer assets required to enable scalable, commercial UAS operations.

What StrataNode does

We design, de-risk, and structure the integrated physical and digital ground-layer assets required to enable scalable, commercial UAS operations. Projects prioritize physical infrastructure first, regulatory compliance second, and commercial viability third.

What StrataNode does not do

We do not manufacture aircraft, operate logistics fleets, or develop standalone UTM software packages. Our focus remains strictly bounded to ground-layer network origination.

2. Why Now

The policy is advancing. The ground layer remains immature.

Policy Transitions

Following the launch of the National Policy Framework for Unmanned Aircraft Systems in August 2025, Ireland has shifted from regulatory consultation into active policy implementation.

Regulatory Credibility

Ireland maintains a highly credible regulatory environment. In 2021, the IAA issued Skyports a BVLOS-capable Light UAS Operator Certificate (LUC) described as the first such certificate issued by an EU regulator.

International Precedent

European drone-corridor initiatives have demonstrated the integration of automated ground stations with secure machine-to-machine peer-to-peer communications. These projects identified station tokenisation, machine payments, trusted telemetry and insurance data as development areas without depending on specific technology providers.

Infrastructure Gap

While operators, regulators, and local authorities build software capabilities, the physical and commercial ground infrastructure layer remains immature, creating a time-sensitive window to secure strategic corridor assets.

9. Corridor Defensibility

Control comes from rights around the corridor—not airspace claims

StrataNode does not claim low-altitude airspace. Long-term commercial defensibility is created entirely through physical, digital, and data rights coordination.

1. Site Rights

Securing exclusive options, long-term leases, or access agreements over critical physical chokepoints, including landing nodes, charging sites, and communication hardware nodes.

2. Corridor-Development Rights

Feasibility and scoping agreements with landowners and partners preserve project-development rights, first-refusal rights, continuing development roles, and permanent SPV equity participation.

3. Digital Coordination Layer

Operating and licensing the core software layer connecting operators, node hosts, charging systems, maintenance providers, and local weather telemetry assets.

4. Trusted Operational Data Layer

Contractual rights to collect, process, anonymise, and commercialise non-personal operational data anchored via decentralized architectures into tamper-proof records for logistics auditing.

5. Project Equity

Extracting structural network value via SPV equity asset ownership, project development milestones, continuing management fees, and multi-operator route access billing.

13. Commercial Structure

A project-specific SPV could ring-fence: contractual liabilities; site and land-access agreements; planning permissions; technology and operator agreements; infrastructure assets; data rights; project financing; and revenue participation. 15. Risks and Policy-Informed Pre-Mortem

What could go wrong? Direct mitigations.

The architecture must survive practical structural, digital, regulatory, and commercial challenges before structuring projects for capital markets.

Market Timing

Policy may shift faster than industrial uptake. Mitigation: Focus completely on high-utility B2B supply corridors where real-world operational ROI offsets early market fragmentation.

Regulatory Delay

Airspace routes face extensive approval backlogs. Mitigation: Design corridors with exceptionally low ground-risk profiles to fit cleanly within existing IAA and EASA SORA parameters.

Network Connectivity and Cyber Disruption

Mobile and fragmented communications environments may create latency, network-handover, telemetry-continuity and cyber-resilience risks. Mitigation: Evaluate redundant communications, secure peer-to-peer networking, edge processing and zero-trust device identity during system design.

Customer Uncertainty

Industrial corporations resist infrastructure locking. Mitigation: Utilize the technical scoping window to build modular pilot data proving clear bottom-line savings before requesting long-term binding SLAs.

Public and Planning Friction

Noise and localized privacy anxieties can halt physical sites. Mitigation: Map corridor utility directly to regional environmental tracking, safety, and community public-good applications.

The Consultancy Trap

StrataNode risks exhausting its resources producing static consulting reports that outside entities commercialize. Mitigation: Ensure that feasibility, scoping and development agreements preserve StrataNode’s potential future role through development rights, site rights, licensing, data rights, management responsibilities, SPV participation or agreed revenue-sharing arrangements.

16. Proposed First Step

One structured corridor-feasibility exercise

60-Day Technical and Spatial Scoping Window

StrataNode intends to map the specific coordinate data and initial ground-risk profiles for a preliminary industrial route linking targeted facilities.

This scoping phase will explicitly include a digital infrastructure blueprint simulating a zero-trust network overlay across the targeted facilities to validate firewall traversal, data latency, and cryptographic node-identity management. It is designed as an industry-led demonstrator matching the milestones of Action 15 of the National Policy Framework.

Institutional guardrail

This document does not state or imply that StrataNode is a state-appointed delivery vehicle, nor does it claim privileged regulatory access. StrataNode is positioned strictly as a private-sector infrastructure originator providing the physical real estate assets, digital coordination layers, and compliance frameworks necessary to support the commercial execution of Ireland's public UAS policy objectives.
