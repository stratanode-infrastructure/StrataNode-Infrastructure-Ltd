1. Who StrataNode Is

An independent infrastructure originator

StrataNode designs, de-risks and structures the physical and digital ground-layer assets required to enable scalable commercial UAS operations.

What StrataNode does

It identifies viable routes, maps the compliance and planning pathway, validates commercial demand and coordinates qualified partners to turn promising opportunities into structured infrastructure projects.

What StrataNode does not do

It is not being developed as an aircraft manufacturer, universal drone operator, construction contractor or standalone UTM software company.

2. Why Now

The policy is advancing. The ground layer remains immature.

Policy transition

Ireland has moved from broad consultation into implementation of a national UAS policy framework that anticipates continued industry participation.

Regulatory capability

Ireland has demonstrated experience in advanced UAS regulation, including early European LUC authorisation supporting BVLOS operations.

Infrastructure gap

Aircraft, software and airspace systems are progressing faster than the physical, commercial and site infrastructure required for repeatable operations.

3. Policy Alignment

Aligned with stated public-policy objectives

StrataNode does not claim to represent the State. Its role is to position private-sector projects so they can support the practical implementation of national and local UAS objectives.

Airspace and planning

Corridor assessment, landing-node planning, geographical-zone considerations and future U-space readiness.

Compliance and safety

A compliance-first pathway covering aviation risk, planning, privacy, insurance, security and public acceptance.

Enterprise and innovation

Industry-led feasibility, demonstrations, test environments and pathways from experimental activity to viable services.

6. What Differentiates StrataNode

An architecture originator, not a single-product drone business

StrataNode’s advantage is not ownership of a particular aircraft, station or software platform. Its role is to define the corridor architecture, preserve project and data rights, establish interoperability standards and coordinate the full enabling stack.

Technology-Agnostic Architecture

Each corridor can adopt the best available aircraft, node, connectivity and data technologies without locking the project into one vendor.

Physical + Digital Integration

Landing nodes, charging, site access, communications, telemetry, booking, data and multi-operator access are coordinated as one infrastructure system.

Defensible Project Rights

Site rights, development rights, data rights, licensing, network-management roles and project equity are designed into feasibility and delivery agreements.

Future-Ready Connectivity

Secure machine identity, zero-trust communications, edge computing, digital twins and trusted telemetry can be introduced where they improve resilience and interoperability.

DePIN Without Speculation

Decentralised infrastructure participation and programmable settlement remain optional utility layers, introduced only where legally compliant and commercially useful.

Recurring Network Economics

The model is designed to support access fees, infrastructure-management fees, data revenues, licensing and long-term project participation rather than one-off consultancy income.

Available today, emerging now and developing next

StrataNode remains technology-agnostic while defining the standards required for secure, interoperable and commercially viable corridor infrastructure.

Emerging Now

  • Cryptographic machine and node identity
  • Zero-trust communications
  • Digital twins and predictive maintenance
  • Dynamic capacity allocation
  • Multi-operator node access
  • Trusted telemetry and automated audit records
Future Utility Layer

DePIN and Programmable Settlement

  • Approved node-host participation
  • Machine-to-machine charging and access payments
  • Transparent revenue allocation
  • Verifiable operational-data markets
  • Cryptographic audit records
  • Tokenised incentives where compliant and justified

Conventional contracts, fiat payments and established governance remain the default during early projects. Blockchain, tokenised or programmable-settlement components would only be introduced where they provide measurable security, interoperability, transparency or commercial benefits and after appropriate legal, tax, accounting, procurement, MiCA and data-protection review.

9. Corridor Defensibility

Control comes from rights around the corridor—not ownership of airspace

StrataNode does not claim low-altitude airspace. Long-term commercial defensibility is created through contractual, physical, digital and data rights.

Site Rights

Options, leases and access agreements over origin and destination nodes, charging sites, rooftops and communications points.

Development Rights

First-refusal, continuing-development, licensing and project-participation rights preserved in feasibility and scoping agreements.

Digital Coordination

Operating or licensing the systems connecting operators, nodes, charging, maintenance, weather and customer access.

Operational Data Rights

Contractual rights to process and commercialise defined non-personal utilisation, reliability, charging and route-performance data.

Project Participation

Development fees, management fees, licensing, data revenue, SPV equity and multi-operator access or booking fees.

11. Pilot Opportunity

Start with one defined industrial route

The first project should test a repeatable point-to-point B2B use case rather than broad consumer delivery.

Clear demand

An identifiable customer and measurable operational or public value.

Controlled sites

Defined origin and destination nodes with realistic access and infrastructure requirements.

Assessable pathway

A licensed operator and a route capable of formal aviation, SORA and planning assessment.

Any conclusion regarding ground risk or regulatory suitability must be confirmed through qualified aviation assessment.

15. Risks and Policy-Informed Pre-Mortem

What could go wrong?

The model must survive practical, regulatory and commercial challenge before it is treated as an investable proposition.

Market timing

Policy and technology may progress faster than paying demand. Early work may therefore focus on feasibility and pilot development.

Regulatory delay

Airspace, SORA, planning, privacy, insurance and engagement requirements may materially extend timelines.

Customer uncertainty

A technically viable corridor is not a business unless a sponsor or customer is willing to fund development and operations.

Public and planning friction

Noise, privacy, safety and perceived surveillance concerns require transparent, benefit-led project selection.

Modular physical works

Value may sit in repeatable multi-site rollout and facilities management rather than one-off heavy-civil construction.

The consultancy trap

StrataNode must retain an agreed future project role or participation right rather than producing reports that others commercialise.

Scope

The project may be described as a potential industry-led demonstrator aligned with the objectives of Ireland’s National Policy Framework for UAS. This does not imply government appointment, regulatory approval or State endorsement.
